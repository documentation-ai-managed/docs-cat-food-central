---

Crude fat is the total amount of fat in the food, including all lipid components.
This measurement is obtained using ether extraction, which isolates the fat content from the rest of the food.
The term "crude" indicates that the measurement includes all types of fats without distinguishing between different fatty acids or their sources.

Example:

- A dry cat food bag may list "crude fat: 15%."
This percentage represents the total fat content in the food, derived from various ingredients.

## Importance of Fat for Cats

Fat plays several critical roles in a cat’s diet:

- **Energy Source:** Fat is a highly concentrated energy source, providing more than twice the calories per gram compared to proteins or carbohydrates.
- **Essential Fatty Acids:** Fats supply essential fatty acids, such as omega-3 and omega-6, necessary for healthy skin, coat, and cellular functions.
- **Palatability:** Fats enhance the taste and texture of food, making it more appealing to cats.
- **Nutrient Absorption:** Fats absorb fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, and K).

Example:

- For an active adult cat, a food with 15-20% crude fat provides sufficient energy and essential fatty acids to support an active lifestyle.

## Sources of Crude Fat

The quality of crude fat depends on its source.
High-quality fats typically come from animal sources, which are more suitable for a cat’s diet than plant-based fats.

- **Animal-based Fats:** Chicken fat, fish oil, beef fat, pork fat.
- **Plant-based Fats:** Vegetable oils, soybean oil, flaxseed oil.

Example:

- A high-quality dry cat food might list chicken fat or fish oil as a primary fat source, ensuring a higher quality of crude fat.

## Interpreting Crude Fat in Dry Cat Food

Although the percentage of crude fat on a pet food label is important, it’s also crucial to consider the source and quality of that fat.

- **High-Quality Fats:** Look for specific animal fats or oils.
- **Lower-Quality Fats:** Be cautious of generic terms like "animal fat" or plant-based fats listed high in the ingredient list.

Example:

- Food A: "Chicken fat, fish oil" - High-quality animal fat sources.
- Food B: "Vegetable oil, soybean oil" - Lower-quality plant-based fats.

## Dry Matter Basis Comparison

To accurately compare the fat content of different foods, it’s important to convert the "as fed" values to a dry matter basis, which excludes moisture content.

### The formula for Calculating Dry Matter

The dry matter amount (A) in percentage is calculated using the following formula:

Dry matter % = ("As fed" nutrient % / (100% - Moisture %)) x 100%

For example, if a dry food contains 15% fat and 10% moisture on an "as fed" basis, the dry matter fat would be:

Dry matter protein % = (15% / (100% - 10%)) x 100% = 16.7%

## Bottom Line

- **Read Labels Carefully:** Look beyond the crude fat percentage and examine the sources.
- **Prefer Animal Fats:** Choose foods with high-quality animal-based fats listed first.
- **Balance Fat Levels:** Ensure the fat content is appropriate for your cat’s age, weight, and activity level.
- **Consult a Vet:** Regular dietary reviews with a veterinarian can help ensure optimal cat fat intake.

    Signs of too much fat can include weight gain and obesity, while too little fat may result in a dull coat, dry skin, and a lack of energy. It's important to consult a veterinarian to determine the appropriate fat levels for your cat based on age, weight, and activity level.

  <Expandable title="Are there specific signs of fat deficiency in cats?">
    Yes, signs of fat deficiency in cats can include a lack of energy, poor coat condition, dry and flaky skin, and a weakened immune system. Essential fatty acid deficiency can also lead to poor growth in kittens and reproductive issues in adult cats.
  </Expandable>

  <Expandable title="Is it okay to supplement my cat's diet with additional fats like fish oil?">
    Supplementing with additional fats, such as fish oil, can be beneficial, especially for cats with specific health concerns like skin issues or arthritis. However, it should be done under the guidance of a veterinarian to avoid over-supplementation, which can lead to an imbalance in the diet.
  </Expandable>

  <Expandable title="How does the crude fat percentage affect cats with special dietary needs, like those with pancreatitis or obesity?">
    Cats with conditions like pancreatitis or obesity often require diets with controlled fat levels. High fat can exacerbate these conditions, so it's crucial to choose a food with an appropriate crude fat percentage and seek veterinary guidance for a tailored dietary plan.
  </Expandable>

  <Expandable title="Do all cats require the same crude fat levels, or does it vary by life stage?">
    No. The required crude fat levels vary depending on the cat's life stage. Kittens and active adult cats may need higher fat content for growth and energy, while senior or less active cats may require lower fat levels to prevent weight gain. Always choose a diet that aligns with your cat's specific life stage and lifestyle.
